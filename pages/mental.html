Postpartum Mental Health

This is for you new moms..


Becoming a mother brings about various changes and sacrifices, such as sleep, exercise, warm meals, and uninterrupted showers. However, the glimpses of motherhood portrayed on social media, with tired moms sipping cold coffee and dealing with leaky boobs, only scratch the surface. The true state of mental health for new moms, especially in the first year, remains largely unshared. Courtney Amerin and Katie Danielson, creators of the PUSH Revolution podcast, stress the importance of preparing for the postpartum period alongside the birth plan. While much attention is given to birth preparations, the postpartum period spans the entire first year and requires a different level of support for both the body and mind.

Expectant parents often educate themselves on the symptoms of postpartum depression, and women are screened for potential mental health challenges. However, once thrust into the fourth trimester, recognizing the signs is not always straightforward. Danielson notes that postpartum mental health challenges extend beyond depression and can include anxiety, PTSD, OCD, and, in rare cases, postpartum psychosis. Most women experience a shift in mood within a few days after giving birth, known as the "baby blues," which usually subside after two weeks. However, for some, feelings of sadness and depression persist, leading to postpartum depression. Statistics indicate that one in eight women experience depression within the first six months after delivery. It's evident that every mother will face some degree of mental health challenge due to hormonal changes, identity shifts, sleep deprivation, and adjusting to caring for a newborn.


A guide to prepare you for your postpartum journey

1. Arrange talk therapy sessions

The postpartum experience encompasses two contrasting truths: the overwhelming love and excitement of having a new baby, and the grueling challenges of caring for and adjusting to motherhood. It is essential to have conversations and seek therapy as a cornerstone of the healing process. Engaging in therapy allows for emotional processing, addressing birth trauma, and promoting self-care. Involving a partner in therapy can provide additional support and equip couples with tools to navigate this transformative phase.

2. Build a strong support system

Assemble your postpartum support network: therapist, pelvic floor physio, massage therapist, pediatrician, personal trainer, and lactation consultant. Research and collect their information for convenient access, ensuring they align with your needs and preferences during this transformative time.

3. Recover your body, restore your mind.

Pregnancy and childbirth bring changes to the body, but regaining strength is possible. Manage expectations and opt for postpartum-specific workouts, whether online or in your community. Consider using apps like Juna, offering expert-designed workouts for safe and healthy postnatal recovery.

4. Discuss important topics with your partner.

Prepare for the changes in your relationship when becoming new parents. Prioritize important conversations beforehand, covering topics like sleep deprivation, sharing responsibilities, self-care needs, and managing household tasks. Clear communication is key as you navigate the new-baby phase together.

5. Prioritize personal space and self-care.

Caring for a newborn is tough, and becoming a mother involves a significant identity shift. It's normal to feel discomfort and grieve your previous self. Dr. Alexandra Sacks likens this transition to puberty, urging self-reflection and acceptance. Journaling, meditating, and reflecting on the changes can aid in embracing motherhood's challenges and transformations.

6. Create a supportive community of fellow moms.

Modern communities no longer provide the support they once did, leaving new parents feeling isolated. However, you can create your own village by reaching out to fellow moms who can assist you in moments when you need a break. Cultivate a support system through apps like Peanut and local community spaces offering playgroups and gatherings. Dispelling the supermom stereotype is crucial for a new mom's mental health. Embrace the idea of leaning on others and find solace in the camaraderie of moms facing similar challenges. Remember, it's okay to ask for help and rely on your newfound village during the beautiful chaos of new motherhood.
