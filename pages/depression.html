Postpartum Depression

What is Postpartum Depression?

Postpartum depression is a condition that some women experience after having a baby. It involves different changes in their bodies, feelings, and actions. The diagnosis is based on how serious and long-lasting the depression is and when it starts, usually within four weeks after giving birth.

The depression is connected to various factors. One is the quick drop in hormones after delivery. Hormones are chemicals in the body that affect how we feel. During pregnancy, hormone levels go up a lot, but they drop quickly after giving birth. This sudden change in hormones might be linked to depression, but we're still not sure exactly how.

Other factors are the social and emotional changes that come with having a baby. Taking care of a newborn can be challenging, and it can affect a woman's mood and well-being.

Most new mothers feel a bit down after giving birth, which is called the "baby blues." But about one out of ten women will experience a more serious and longer-lasting depression after having a baby. In rare cases, some women develop a condition called postpartum psychosis, which is more severe.

It's important to know that dads can also get depressed after their baby is born. About one in ten new fathers experience depression during the first year of their child's life.

Treatment for postpartum depression usually involves taking medication and talking to a counselor or therapist who can provide support and guidance.

Postpartum Depression Signs and Symptoms

Postpartum depression can be difficult to notice, but many women experience these signs after giving birth:

  • Trouble sleeping.
  • Changes in appetite.
  • Feeling extremely tired.
  • Decreased interest in pleasure.
  • Mood swings.

With postpartum depression, these symptoms are accompanied by other signs of major depression that are not typical after childbirth. These can include:

  • Feeling uninterested in or disconnected from the baby.
  • Crying often without a clear reason.
  • Feeling consistently sad.
  • Being extremely angry or irritable.
  • Losing interest in things you used to enjoy.
  • Feeling worthless, hopeless, or helpless.
  • Having thoughts of death or suicide.
  • Having thoughts of hurting someone else.
  • Difficulty concentrating or making decisions.

In some cases, women may also experience symptoms of obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) or panic disorder alongside depression.

It's important for new moms to seek help from a professional when:

  • Symptoms persist for more than two weeks.
  • They are unable to function normally.
  • Everyday situations become overwhelming.
  • They have thoughts of harming themselves or their baby.
  • They feel extremely anxious, scared, or panicked most of the day.

Causes and Risk Factors of Postpartum Depression

Postpartum depression is not your fault, and it can happen for various reasons. Some factors that increase the chances of experiencing postpartum depression include:

  • Having a history of depression before or during pregnancy.
  • Being young when you become pregnant.
  • Feeling unsure or conflicted about the pregnancy.
  • Having multiple children, as the risk may increase with each pregnancy.
  • Having a family history of mood disorders.
  • Going through highly stressful events like job loss or health problems.
  • Having a child with special needs or health issues.
  • Giving birth to twins or triplets.
  • Having a history of depression or premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD).
  • Having limited social support.
  • Living alone.
  • Experiencing conflict in your marriage or relationship.

There isn't a specific cause for postpartum depression, but certain physical and emotional factors may contribute to it:

  • Hormonal changes:
    After giving birth, there is a significant drop in estrogen and progesterone levels, which can affect your mood. Thyroid hormone levels may also decrease, leading to feelings of fatigue, sluggishness, and depression.

  • Lack of sleep:
    When you are sleep-deprived and overwhelmed, even small problems can feel challenging to handle.

  • Anxiety:
    Worries about your ability to take care of your newborn can contribute to postpartum depression.

  • Self-image:
    Feeling less attractive, struggling with your sense of identity, or feeling a loss of control over your life can all play a role in postpartum depression.

Types of Postpartum Depression

There are three terms used to describe the mood changes women can have after giving birth:

  • Baby Blues:
    happens to as many as 70% of women in the days right after childbirth. You may have sudden mood swings, such as feeling very happy and then feeling very sad. You may cry for no reason and can feel impatient, cranky, restless, anxious, lonely, and sad. The baby blues may last only a few hours or as long as 1 to 2 weeks after delivery. Usually you don’t need treatment from a health care provider for baby blues. Often, joining a support group of new moms or talking with other moms helps.

  • Postpartum depression (PPD):
    can happen a few days or even months after childbirth. PPD can happen after the birth of any child, not just the first child. You can have feelings similar to the baby blues -- sadness, despair, anxiety, crankiness -- but you feel them much more strongly.
    PPD often keeps you from doing the things you need to do every day. When your ability to function is affected, you need to see a health care provider, such as your OB/GYN or primary care doctor. This doctor can screen you for depression symptoms and come up with a treatment plan. If you don’t get treatment for PPD, symptoms can get worse. While PPD is a serious condition, it can be treated with medication and counseling.

  • Postpartum psychosis:
    is a very serious mental illness that can affect new mothers. This illness can happen quickly, often within the first 3 months after childbirth.
    Women can lose touch with reality, having auditory hallucinations (hearing things that aren't actually happening, like a person talking) and delusions (strongly believing things that are clearly irrational).
    Visual hallucinations (seeing things that aren't there) are less common.
    Other symptoms include insomnia (not being able to sleep), feeling agitated and angry, pacing, restlessness, and strange feelings and behaviors.
    Women who have postpartum psychosis need treatment right away and almost always need medication. Sometimes women are put into the hospital because they are at risk for hurting themselves or someone else.

Postpartum Depression Treatment

Postpartum depression is treated differently depending on the symptoms and their severity.
The treatment options include:

  • Medications:
    Doctors may prescribe anti-anxiety or antidepressant medications to help improve the mood and manage symptoms.

  • Psychotherapy:
    Talk therapy, such as counseling or psychotherapy, can be beneficial. It involves talking to a mental health professional who can provide support, guidance, and strategies to cope with postpartum depression.

  • Support groups:
    Joining a support group for new mothers can provide emotional support and education about postpartum depression. Sharing experiences with others who understand what you're going through can be helpful.

In the case of postpartum psychosis, additional medications used to treat psychosis may be prescribed. Hospital admission is often necessary to ensure the safety of the mother and others.

If you are breastfeeding, it's important to have a conversation with your doctor. Many medications used to treat postpartum depression, anxiety, or psychosis can be taken while breastfeeding, under a doctor's supervision.

The treatment approach will be determined based on your individual situation, and it's important to work closely with your healthcare provider to find the most suitable treatment plan.

Postpartum Depression Complications

If postpartum depression is left untreated, it can lead to complications that affect both you and your family:

  • For you:
    Untreated postpartum depression can last for a long time, even turning into a chronic depressive disorder. Even with treatment, it increases the chances of experiencing depression episodes in the future.

  • For the baby's father:
    When a new mother has depression, the father is more likely to experience depression as well.

  • For children:
    Children of mothers with postpartum depression may have difficulties with sleeping and eating, excessive crying, and delays in language development. It can impact their overall well-being and development.

How can we prevent Postpartum Depression?

To help prevent postpartum depression, there are steps you can take during pregnancy and after your baby is born

During pregnancy:

  • Inform your doctor if you have a history of depression. They can monitor you closely and provide appropriate support.
  • Seek support from support groups, counseling, or other therapies to manage mild depression symptoms. Your doctor may also prescribe medications if necessary, even during pregnancy.
  • Feeling unsure or conflicted about the pregnancy.

Childbirth:

  • Attend a postpartum checkup with your doctor to screen for symptoms of depression. Early diagnosis allows for early treatment.
  • If you have a history of postpartum depression, your doctor may recommend starting treatment immediately after giving birth.

Tips for managing after childbirth:

  • Don't hesitate to ask for help from others. Let them know how they can assist you.
  • Set realistic expectations for yourself and your baby.
  • Engage in gentle exercise within the limits advised by your doctor, such as taking walks.
  • Anticipate good and bad days.
  • Maintain a healthy diet, avoiding alcohol and excessive caffeine.
  • Nurture your relationship with your partner by making time for each other.
  • Stay connected with family and friends to avoid isolating yourself.
  • Limit the number of visitors when you first come home.
  • Prioritize rest and sleep, taking advantage of moments when your baby is sleeping.

By following these steps, you can take proactive measures to reduce the risk of postpartum depression and support your well-being during the postpartum period.
